## The Myth of the Infinite Ground

Every beginner learns that ground is ground — a zero-voltage reference, a place to sink current, an afterthought. It's the net you pour copper into and stop thinking about.

That mental model will fail you the moment your board has more than one switching node.

## Return Currents Follow the Path of Least Impedance

At DC, that path is the path of least resistance. At frequency — and your digital signals are always at frequency — that path is the path of least inductance, which means it follows directly beneath the signal trace.

This is the core insight: a current flowing through a trace on layer 1 creates a return current in the ground plane on layer 2 that mirrors the signal's path as closely as the geometry allows.

## What Happens When You Break the Plane

A slot, a cutout, an anti-pad — anything that interrupts the return path forces the current to route around the obstacle. The loop area expands. Loop area is a loop antenna. You've just built a radiator into your board.

This is why you never route signals across a plane split, even if the signal net has no electrical connection to the split. The return current doesn't know about nets. It knows about impedance.

## Practical Implications

Keep your ground plane solid under every high-frequency trace. When you must split planes for analog/digital isolation, route the crossing signals over the bridge point — the only place where the return path is continuous across the split.

Place decoupling capacitors at every power pin, and make sure their via return path is short. A cap ten millimeters from its power pin via is not a decoupling capacitor — it's a stub.

The board is a circuit. The planes are conductors. Design them accordingly.
